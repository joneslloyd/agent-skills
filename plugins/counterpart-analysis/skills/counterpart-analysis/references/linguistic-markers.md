# Linguistic Markers Reference

The "X phrasing hints at Y" catalogue. Every entry follows the same discipline: **marker → plausible reading(s) → strength → common false positives**. No single marker proves anything. Signal lives in (a) clusters of converging markers, (b) deviations from the person's own baseline, and (c) incongruence between content and register.

## 1. Pronoun patterns

| Marker | Plausible reading | Strength |
|---|---|---|
| Heavy "I/me/my" on decisions | Speaker may lack final authority (real decider elsewhere) — or is genuinely the decider and owns it; disambiguate via whether commitments actually stick | Weak alone |
| Heavy "we/our team/they" | Representing group consensus, or diffusing personal accountability into the collective | Weak alone |
| **Shift** from "I" to "we" mid-conversation | Often marks the moment they start deflecting to higher authority or distancing from a position they held personally | Medium — shifts beat states |
| **Shift** from "we" to "I" | Taking personal ownership — often precedes a genuine commitment or a personal appeal | Medium |
| "You" appearing in blame contexts ("you were supposed to...") | Accountability positioning; building a record | Medium in written channels |
| Dropped pronouns entirely ("Was agreed that...", "Decision was made") | Agentless framing around contested decisions — who agreed? Classic accountability diffusion | Medium–Strong when clustered with passives |

**How to use:** if "I" is heavy, the calibrated question is "How does your team/leadership weigh in on this?" If "we" is heavy, verify authority before treating any yes as final.

## 2. Commitment language

Rank-order of commitment strength. Downgrades along this ladder — especially between drafts, meetings, or thread turns — are among the most reliable tells available.

1. "I will do X by [date]" — full commitment
2. "We will do X" — commitment, diffused owner
3. "We plan to / we intend to" — direction, not promise
4. "We should be able to" — hedged capability claim
5. "We'll try / we'll do our best" — pre-positioned excuse; expect slippage
6. "We'll look into it / take it away / circle back" — often a soft no or a parking move
7. "That's an interesting idea / worth exploring" — frequently a polite decline in corporate register
8. Silence on the item — see §6; often the strongest "no" of all

| Marker | Plausible reading | Strength |
|---|---|---|
| "Yes" without any implementation detail | Counterfeit yes — appeasement to end the conversation; real agreement usually volunteers a how | Medium; test with "how will your team handle...?" |
| Enthusiastic yes + spontaneous detail ("yes — I'll get Priya to set up the repo Monday") | Commitment yes; intent is real | Medium–Strong |
| Agreement that shrinks on repetition (Rule of Three: affirm 3×, 3 ways) | The deal was never real; watch which element they drop | Strong |
| Conditional stacking ("assuming X, and if Y, we could possibly...") | Building an exit corridor before agreeing | Medium |
| Precise numbers ($47,235; "by the 14th") | Feels calculated/final; in negotiation, signals (or performs) a hard limit | Medium |
| Suspiciously round numbers with "final" framing | Usually not a real limit; there is room | Medium |

## 3. Hedging and qualifiers

| Marker | Plausible reading | Strength |
|---|---|---|
| "Sort of / kind of / basically / more or less" clustered around one topic | Discomfort or low confidence localized to that topic — the cluster's *location* is the datum | Medium when localized |
| "To be honest / frankly / candidly" | Discourse marker preceding something they consider risky to say — attend to what follows; also flags that surrounding statements were more guarded | Weak–Medium |
| "I think / I believe / it seems" from a normally assertive speaker | Baseline break: genuine uncertainty, or softening bad news | Medium (only vs. baseline) |
| "Obviously / clearly / everyone knows" | Pre-empting challenge on a point that is not obvious; often the weakest link in their argument | Medium |
| "Just" as minimizer ("just checking", "just a small ask") | Managing imposition — the ask usually matters more than "just" admits | Weak alone; strong in incongruence clusters |
| "Honestly, it's fine / it's not a big deal" unprompted | Nobody said it was a big deal; unsolicited minimization often marks the actual big deal | Medium |

## 4. Distancing language

People unconsciously add linguistic distance between themselves and things they're uncomfortable with.

| Marker | Plausible reading | Strength |
|---|---|---|
| Passive voice around accountability ("mistakes were made", "the deadline was missed") | Agent-deletion; ownership avoidance — note *which* events get passivized while others stay active | Medium–Strong as a pattern |
| "That project / the situation / this whole thing" replacing a previously named item | Demonstrative distancing; the item became emotionally or politically loaded | Medium |
| Referring to a person by role/title after previously using their name | Relationship cooling or formal-record positioning | Medium |
| Abstract nouns replacing verbs ("there were communication challenges" vs. "I didn't tell you") | Nominalization hides actors; standard in blame-adjacent corporate prose | Medium as pattern |
| Third-personing themselves ("as the project lead, one has to...") | Performing a role rather than speaking personally; watch what triggers it | Medium |

## 5. Specificity gradients

Where detail lives — and where it dies — maps the terrain of comfort.

| Marker | Plausible reading | Strength |
|---|---|---|
| Rich detail on topic A, sudden vagueness on topic B | B is where the problem, secret, or undecided thing lives | **Strong** — one of the best structural tells |
| Vague on the past, specific on the future (or vice versa) | Direction of avoidance: past-vague suggests covering; future-vague suggests no real plan or no real intention | Medium |
| Over-detailed account of something trivial | Rehearsed narrative or anxiety displacement; also just a rambler — check baseline | Weak–Medium |
| Numbers without sources ("costs went up 30%") when other claims are sourced | The unsourced number is the one doing persuasion work | Medium |

## 6. Deflection and non-answers

| Marker | Plausible reading | Strength |
|---|---|---|
| Answering a different, adjacent question | The real answer is unfavorable, unknown, or unsharable; the *gap* between asked and answered is the datum | **Strong** when repeated on the same topic |
| Answering with a question | Buying time, testing your position before revealing theirs, or genuine curiosity — sequence matters | Medium |
| "That's a great question" + generalities | Stalling; no prepared answer or unwilling to give it | Medium |
| Reframing your question before answering ("I think what you're really asking is...") | Steering to preferred ground; note what got dropped in the reframe | Medium–Strong |
| Humor/self-deprecation exactly at pressure points | Tension release and topic escape; if the same topic keeps getting joked away, that topic is live | Medium as pattern |
| In email: replying to 3 of 4 points | The ignored point is load-bearing. List every ignored point explicitly in the evidence layer | **Strong** |

## 7. Unsolicited content

What people volunteer maps their preoccupations more reliably than what they answer.

| Marker | Plausible reading | Strength |
|---|---|---|
| Unsolicited denial ("this isn't about budget, but...") | The negated frame was on their mind; often the actual frame | Medium–Strong |
| Unprompted justification of a decision nobody challenged | They expect challenge; the decision is contested somewhere (possibly internally) | Medium |
| Volunteering constraints early ("before we start, I should say we're very limited on X") | Anchoring/expectation-setting, or genuine transparency — either way, X is central to their model of the conversation | Medium |
| Mentioning alternatives unprompted ("we're also talking to...") | Leverage display; may be real or performed — probe with "what would help you decide?" | Medium |
| Repeatedly raising a topic that keeps getting parked | That topic is their actual agenda regardless of the official one | **Strong** |

## 8. Emphasis and protest markers

| Marker | Plausible reading | Strength |
|---|---|---|
| Over-affirmation ("absolutely, 100%, no problem at all") | Enthusiasm or overcompensation — check whether follow-through language (the *how*) accompanies it | Weak alone |
| "As I said / as I already mentioned / per my last email" | Frustration + record-building; the relationship is degrading into documentation | Medium–Strong in written channels |
| Superlatives clustering ("hugely important", "top priority", "critical") on one item | Either it truly is, or they're selling; check whether resource commitments match the adjectives | Medium |
| Protesting too much (repeated unprompted assurances of honesty/simplicity/alignment) | Each repetition of an unrequested assurance raises the odds the assured thing is in doubt | Medium as pattern |

## 9. Authority invocation

| Marker | Plausible reading | Strength |
|---|---|---|
| "Leadership / legal / finance has concerns" (unnamed) | Real constraint, borrowed authority for their own preference, or a shield for a decision already made — distinguish by asking what specifically the concern is; real constraints come with specifics | Medium |
| Named authority ("Sarah in legal flagged clause 4") | Usually a real, checkable constraint | — |
| "My hands are tied" | Either true (find the rope: "what would need to be true for this to work?") or a negotiation posture | Medium |
| Sudden appearance of authority mid-negotiation ("I'll have to run this by...") after earlier autonomy | Classic higher-authority gambit to extract concessions, or genuine scope discovery — the *timing* relative to your last ask is the tell | Medium–Strong |
| Speaking *for* absent parties' emotions ("the team is really upset") | Unverifiable proxy claims; may be accurate, may be ventriloquism for their own position | Medium |

## 10. Fairness and values language

| Marker | Plausible reading | Strength |
|---|---|---|
| "We just want what's fair" | Guilt lever; implies you've been unfair. Respond by making it concrete: "I want to be fair too — have I done something unfair? Let's discuss it" | Medium |
| "This is a fair offer" | Anchoring; a bid to close the question. Mirror ("Fair?"), then "How did you arrive at that figure?" | Medium |
| Appeals to relationship history ("after everything we've done together") | Normative leverage — invoking obligations; often deployed when the substantive case is weak | Medium |
| Their own stated values quoted back at themselves later ("you've always said quality comes first") | Someone using normative leverage *on you or others* — note who wields it | — |
| Sudden principle-talk in a previously pragmatic negotiation | Position weakening on the merits; retreating to higher ground | Medium |

## 11. Temporal patterns

| Marker | Plausible reading | Strength |
|---|---|---|
| Urgency without stated reason ("we need this signed this week") | Real external deadline, manufactured pressure, or something expiring you can't see. Always ask: whose deadline is it, and what happens if it passes? | Medium |
| Deadline that keeps moving when tested | Manufactured | Strong once observed |
| Past tense creeping into present arrangements ("it *was* a good partnership", "what we *had* planned") | Mental disengagement may already have happened | Medium–Strong |
| Future tense evaporating (no more "next quarter we'll...") from someone who used to plan aloud | Horizon shortening; disengagement or known-but-unannounced change | Medium |
| Response latency shifts in async channels (same-hour replier → 3-day replier) on one thread while active elsewhere | Deprioritization, avoidance, or off-channel consultation about you | Medium — verify they're active elsewhere before reading in |

## 12. Register shifts

The most information-dense category. A register shift means *something changed* — locating the pivot point in the thread/transcript is often the whole analysis.

| Marker | Plausible reading | Strength |
|---|---|---|
| Warm → formal ("Hi Lloyd! :)" → "Dear Lloyd,") | Cooling, escalation prep, lawyer/HR consultation, or new invisible audience (forwarding) | **Strong**; locate the pivot event |
| Casual → precise/legalistic vocabulary ("as per our agreement", "pursuant to") | Record-building; they anticipate dispute | **Strong** |
| Long collaborative replies → terse acknowledgments ("Noted." "Fine.") | Withdrawal, anger, or capitulation-in-form-only; expect the topic to resurface via another route | Medium–Strong |
| Sudden politeness spike after conflict | Genuine repair, or strategic repositioning before an ask/escalation — watch the next message | Medium |
| Exclamation marks and emoji vanishing (vs. that person's baseline) | Effort/effect withdrawal | Weak–Medium, baseline-dependent |

## 13. Email- and chat-specific signals

Structural metadata often outweighs the prose.

- **CC additions**: adding a manager = escalation or accountability theater; adding legal = dispute anticipated; adding their own team = internal cover. Ask: *who is this email really written for?* Prose addressed to you but performed for the CC line reads differently — stiffer, more complete, more self-justifying.
- **CC removals / moving someone to BCC**: taking the conversation private; the removed person is relevant to what comes next.
- **Reply-all vs. direct reply** choices around sensitive points.
- **Forwarding artifacts** ("FYI ↓") — you are seeing a conversation about the conversation.
- **Thread hijacking**: replying to an old thread to raise a new topic — links the new ask to the old context deliberately (precedent-setting) or hides it from fresh-thread visibility.
- **Timing**: 11pm emails from a 9-to-5 sender = pressure or performance of pressure; Friday-5pm delivery of bad news = burial attempt.
- **Subject-line edits** mid-thread ("RE: Project X" → "RE: Project X — URGENT — decision needed") = state change plus record-building.
- **Chat-specific**: typing-then-stopping (composing and abandoning), long pauses before short answers, "..." as pressure, reactions (👍) replacing replies as engagement withdrawal, precision-quoting your message back (record-building even in Slack).

## 14. Meeting-transcript-specific signals

- **Interruption patterns**: who interrupts whom, and who *gets* interrupted without objection — live status map.
- **Silence after a proposal**: who spoke first and what they said; the first response often gets anchoring deference.
- **"Let's take this offline"**: topic suppression — note who moved to suppress and which topic; the offline list is frequently the real agenda.
- **Agreement choreography**: who looks to whom before agreeing (visible in video, inferable from turn order in transcripts — B consistently agreeing only after C speaks).
- **Pre-meeting and post-meeting small talk** (if captured): the unguarded minutes; disproportionately informative.
- **Question ratios**: participants who only ask questions and never state positions are gathering intelligence or hiding their stance; participants who never ask questions may have already decided.
- **Who summarizes**: the person who "captures what we agreed" is exercising framing power — compare their summary with what was actually said; deltas are deliberate more often than chance.

## 15. Channel calibration

Baseline registers differ by medium; recalibrate before reading anything as a deviation.

- **Email** is systematically more formal and hedged than speech; softeners are house style, not signal, unless they cluster incongruently or shift over a thread.
- **Chat** (Slack/Teams) is systematically terse; brevity is not coldness. Signal lives in *changes* within the same relationship.
- **Transcribed speech** is messier than people's considered views — disfluencies, false starts, and self-corrections are normal cognition, not deception markers. Weight structural moves (deflection, topic control, specificity) over fluency.
- **Non-native speakers**: hedging, unusual directness/indirectness, tense inconsistencies, and formality misfires are ESL artifacts before they are tells. Downweight all register markers heavily; retain structural markers (what they answer, ignore, volunteer, and repeat), which survive translation.
- **Regulated/legal/pharma corporate registers**: passive voice, agentless constructions, and hedges are compliance culture. Baseline within the org's own documents, not against general English.

## 16. False-positive table

Consult before finalizing any hypothesis. Each row is a boring explanation that must be beaten, not ignored.

| Observation | Suspicious reading | Boring reading that often wins |
|---|---|---|
| Short, delayed replies | Avoidance, anger | Busy; mobile; different email culture |
| Heavy hedging | Concealment | Junior, anxious, non-native, or scientist/lawyer trained to hedge |
| Passive voice | Blame dodging | House style; genuinely unknown agent |
| "We" everywhere | No authority | Team-oriented culture; genuine consensus process |
| No answer to one point | Strategic omission | Read on phone, missed it (once); strategic only if it survives a re-ask |
| Formal register | Cooling | New job, new counsel-mandated template, cultural norm |
| Urgency | Pressure tactic | Their boss really did ask this morning |
| Enthusiasm without detail | Counterfeit yes | Genuine yes from a non-detail person — check their baseline for ever giving detail |
| Contradiction between meetings | Deception | Changed circumstances; they learned something; ordinary human inconsistency |

**Decision rule:** a marker enters a hypothesis only if it (a) clusters with independent markers, (b) deviates from that person's own baseline, or (c) survives one boring explanation stated explicitly in the output.
