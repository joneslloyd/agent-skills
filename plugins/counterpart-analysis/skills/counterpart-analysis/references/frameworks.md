## 1. Interests vs. positions

A **position** is what someone says they want. An **interest** is why they want it. Positions conflict far more often than interests do, and positions are frequently poor proxies for the interests underneath.

- "We need the price down 20%" (position) might be driven by: a budget cap someone imposed (constraint), needing a visible "win" to report (status), genuine cash pressure (survival), or standard opening theater (process).
- The same position can serve opposite interests. "Let's delay the launch" can mean *protect quality* or *starve the project until it dies quietly*.

**Method:** for every stated position in the material, generate 2–4 candidate interests, then use the evidence layer to score them. The candidate the person's *questions, omissions, and volunteered content* point to usually beats the candidate their *statements* point to.

**Shared and compatible interests matter as much as opposed ones** — the analysis should surface where the target's inferred interests align with the user's, because that is where the practical moves in Step 5 come from.

## 2. The interest taxonomy

Check every layer; people are rarely driven by only one.

| Layer | Typical contents | Common tells |
|---|---|---|
| **Professional/metric** | KPIs, deadlines, budget, headcount, quarterly targets | Precise dates/numbers they keep returning to; urgency spikes near quarter boundaries |
| **Political/organizational** | Credit, blame-avoidance, turf, alliance-building, precedent | CC-line engineering; "alignment" vocabulary; who they invoke and who they never mention |
| **Personal** | Workload, job security, promotion, not looking stupid, going home on time | Resistance disproportionate to the org-level stakes; "bandwidth" talk; enthusiasm that tracks effort-to-them rather than value-to-org |
| **Relational** | Trust history, past grievances, face, reciprocity ledger | History references ("last time...", "after everything..."); ledger-keeping language |
| **Identity/values** | Craft pride, fairness, being the reasonable one, expertise recognition | Principle-talk; correcting details that don't matter materially; "as the person who built this..." |

Universal underlying needs to keep in view: **respect, security (predictability), autonomy (control), recognition**. When a reaction seems disproportionate to the material stakes, one of these four was touched.

## 3. Black Swans: the three hidden-information types

Assume roughly three game-changing unknowns exist in any substantive situation. They come in three types:

1. **Secret constraints** — "my boss capped this at X", a contract clause, a hiring freeze, a compliance rule they can't cite. *Signature:* firm, oddly shaped limits defended without substantive argument; positions that won't move even when their stated rationale is satisfied.
2. **Hidden motivations** — this deal saves their job; they're leaving in two months; they sponsored the competing internal project. *Signature:* energy misallocated relative to stated goals; caring too much or too little; interest in provisions that shouldn't matter to their stated role.
3. **Unknown context** — a reorg you haven't heard about, a competitor move, a parallel negotiation. *Signature:* sudden strategy changes with no visible trigger; new vocabulary appearing (they've been in meetings you weren't in); timeline shifts.

**Hunting method in static material:** collect anomalies — overreactions, hesitations, non-sequiturs, topic-avoidance clusters (linguistic-markers §5, §6), and behavior that seems irrational. Each anomaly is a candidate Black Swan trailhead. Then design a discriminating question (§11) rather than guessing.

## 4. The irrationality diagnostic

When the target's behavior seems irrational, do NOT conclude bad faith. Run the three-way diagnostic in order:

1. **Ill-informed** — they lack information you have. Predicts: their position should move when the information is supplied. Cheapest to test; test it first.
2. **Constrained** — hidden limits (§3.1) make their behavior rational within a boundary you can't see. Predicts: they'll engage warmly on everything *except* the constrained dimension.
3. **Concealing** — they have an interest they don't want visible. Predicts: deflection clusters, specificity drops, and incongruence localized around one region of the topic space.

Only assign (3) after (1) and (2) fail to explain the evidence — and say in the output that you did so. Analyses that jump straight to concealment are the single biggest source of paranoid overreads.

## 5. Constraint and authority mapping

For the target, answer:

- **Can they say yes alone?** Evidence: pronoun patterns (I vs. we), whether past commitments from them stuck, whether they ever grant anything on the spot vs. always "coming back to you", invocation timing (does authority appear precisely when concessions are requested?).
- **What can't they say?** Legal/HR/compliance topics, information about other parties (candidates, vendors, prices), anything creating precedent or written commitment. Non-answers in these zones are role-compliance, not personal evasion — classify them as such.
- **Who are they performing for?** Every utterance has an audience. In email: the CC line. In meetings: their manager in the room, or the transcript itself. Statements aimed at an audience other than the user should be read as positioning artifacts, not beliefs. Ask of every strange statement: *for whom is this statement rational?*
- **What would saying yes cost them?** Effort, precedent, political capital, exposure if it fails. A "no" that protects them from downside is not opposition to the user — it's risk management; the practical move is de-risking the yes, not arguing the merits.

## 6. Incentive mapping (principal–agent)

The most predictive single question: **what does success look like for this person in the next 90 days, and who decides whether they achieved it?**

- Identify their probable principal(s): direct manager, a steering committee, a client, their own KPI dashboard.
- People optimize for how their work is *measured and seen*, not for the org's stated goals. Misalignment between the org's interest and the agent's incentive explains most "irrational" organizational behavior without any bad faith.
- Common divergences to check: quarter-end vs. long-term quality; being safe vs. being right ("nobody gets fired for choosing the incumbent"); activity legible to their boss vs. outcomes invisible to their boss; risk of blame (asymmetric — downside personal, upside shared).
- If the material doesn't reveal their incentive structure, name that as the top intelligence gap and put a question targeting it in Step 5 (e.g., "How is this project's success being measured on your side?").

## 7. BATNA inference

Estimate the target's best alternative to agreement (their walkaway option), because their angle is shaped by how good life is for them without the user.

- **Signals of a strong BATNA:** unhurried timelines, comfort with silence/no-reply, casual mention of alternatives, willingness to let issues remain open, price/term insensitivity.
- **Signals of a weak BATNA:** urgency, over-responsiveness, unprompted concessions, relationship appeals substituting for substantive leverage, "fairness" escalation.
- **Caution:** all of these are performable. Displayed alternatives ("we're also talking to two other vendors") are claims, not facts. Distinguish by cost: signals that are *expensive to fake* (actually letting a deadline pass; genuinely disengaging for weeks) beat cheap talk.
- Never infer the user's own strategy solely from the target's displayed BATNA — recommend they compute their own alternatives independently.

## 8. Counterpart styles

Style shapes how the same interest gets expressed. Misreading style as substance is a classic error (e.g., reading an Analyst's slowness as reluctance, or an Accommodator's warmth as agreement).

| Style | Textual signature | Fears | Read/adapt |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Analyst** | Precise, data-anchored, long considered replies, dislikes surprises, sparse small talk | Being rushed; error; unpreparedness | Their silence is processing, not resistance. Vagueness from an Analyst is a genuine red flag (baseline break). Give data and time. |
| **Accommodator** | Warm, relational, quick agreement, conflict-avoidant, generous small talk | Confrontation; damaged relationships | Their "yes" is the least informative yes of the three styles — pin specifics. Their *hesitation* is highly informative (it costs them to show it). |
| **Assertive** | Direct, fast, brief, comfortable with "no", win-framed | Wasted time; looking weak | Their bluntness is not hostility (style, not signal). What *is* signal: an Assertive who suddenly goes quiet or elaborate — major baseline break. |

Calibrate every marker in linguistic-markers.md against style: hedging is signal from an Assertive and noise from an Accommodator; warmth is noise from an Accommodator and signal from an Analyst.

## 9. Leverage triangle

Read which levers the target believes they hold, and which they're using — it reveals their model of the relationship.

- **Positive leverage** — controlling something the other side wants. Usage tell: reminding you of benefits, dangling access/scope/renewals.
- **Negative leverage** — ability to impose costs. Usage tell: consequence-mentioning, deadline weaponization, escalation hints ("I'd hate for this to go to procurement/legal").
- **Normative leverage** — using the other side's stated values/standards against them. Usage tell: quoting your own principles or marketing back at you; fairness framing.

Someone reaching for **negative** leverage early usually reveals a weak positive hand. Someone relying entirely on **normative** leverage usually has neither. The key orienting question in any standoff: *who believes they have more to lose if this falls apart?* — the material usually shows who is acting like it.

## 10. Face, status, and audience

A large fraction of workplace behavior that looks substantive is face-management:

- People will reject good proposals that make them look overruled, and accept mediocre ones they can present as their idea. If the target's resistance tracks *authorship* rather than *content* (they oppose your version, then propose a near-identical one), the interest is credit/face, and the practical move is giving them ownership.
- **Face-saving exits:** when someone has publicly committed to a position the evidence has turned against, they need a bridge (new information, changed circumstances) to move without humiliation. If the analysis concludes the target is trapped in a prior position, Step 5 should include building them that bridge.
- **Status reading in transcripts:** who interrupts, who gets interrupted, who is deferred to, whose jokes land, who summarizes (framing power), who speaks first/last on decisions. Formal org charts and transcript status maps frequently disagree — trust the transcript.

## 11. Designing discriminating tests

A hypothesis without a test is an opinion. Good tests make rival hypotheses predict *different observable responses*.

- **Calibrated questions** (open "how/what"; avoid "why", which reads as accusatory): "How did you arrive at that figure?", "What happens on your side if this slips?", "What about this is important to you?", "How does your team decide something like this?" Each targets a specific unknown (constraint origin, deadline reality, value driver, authority structure).
- **No-oriented questions** — people answer "no" more safely and honestly than "yes": "Would it be unreasonable to...?", "Is it a bad time to...?", "Have you given up on X?" (re-engages silent counterparts and forces a corrective "no" that carries information).
- **Tactical mislabel** — deliberately slightly-wrong label to trigger a correction: "It seems like the budget is the real blocker here?" — the *shape of the correction* is the data.
- **Strawman proposal** — float a small concrete option that H1 and H2 predict different reactions to. E.g., if H1 = "they need a date for leadership" and H2 = "they're building a case to switch vendors", offering an earlier date with a scope trade satisfies H1 and will be strangely unwelcome under H2.
- **Information-drip test** — supply the information that would move an *ill-informed* counterpart (§4). If their position doesn't move, promote constraint/concealment hypotheses.
- **Silence** — in live settings, an unfilled pause after their statement; people negotiate against themselves into vacuums. (Advise the user of this for the next live interaction; it can't be run on static text.)

Ethical line: tests surface information the counterpart is free to give or withhold. Do not design tests that entrap, deceive materially, or exploit disclosed vulnerabilities.

## 12. Hypothesis quality checklist

Run every hypothesis through this before it ships:

- [ ] Stated in one falsifiable sentence (a specific want/constraint, not "they have mixed feelings")
- [ ] Supported by ≥2 *independent* evidence items (two instances of the same marker type = one item)
- [ ] At least one structural evidence item (omission, question pattern, effort asymmetry, metadata), not phrasing alone
- [ ] Every supporting item carries a provenance tag ([OBSERVED]/[CONTEXT]/[INFERRED]/[ASSUMED]); no untagged claims
- [ ] Load-bearing assumptions identified and cross-referenced to the Assumptions register — the reader can see exactly what breaks if each is wrong
- [ ] Any user-supplied characterization the hypothesis relies on is flagged as such ([CONTEXT], one party's account), not treated as ground truth
- [ ] The strongest boring explanation from the false-positive table has been stated and addressed
- [ ] Disconfirming/unexplained evidence listed (if none exists, the hypothesis is too vague)
- [ ] Confidence label justified: High requires convergent multi-type evidence + baseline deviation AND no load-bearing [ASSUMED] items; phrasing-only support caps at Medium; single-email material caps at Medium regardless
- [ ] Has a concrete discriminating test the user can actually run — tests that retire a load-bearing assumption rank first
- [ ] Contains no clinical/diagnostic labels and no claims about the person's character — only about their probable interests and constraints in this situation
